A Brief History of Neuroscience

  • Veena Girijala
  • Jan 1, 2020

I think we can all agree on the fact that the brain is the most significant and complex organ of the human body. The human brain is basically a three-pound paradox that is superior to any other structure in the universe, capable of studying itself. The burgeoning field of neuroscience has led to an almost unprecedented burst of scientific research, reshaping disciplines from biochemistry and personalized medicine to law, psychology and the arts. Meanwhile, newer imaging technologies, like functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), have offered revolutionary ways to view brain activity in real time.

The ancient Egyptians were the first to mention the brain in writing—preserved on a 2,700-year-old papyrus that details the symptoms of head injuries—but the importance of the organ escaped them. They believed it was the heart, not the head, that controlled human thought and emotion. They also believed that while the heart was responsible for intelligence, the function of the brain was to cool the blood in your body. A roman guy, Galen, came along and called foul on this theory and presented a theory that stated the brain is the hub all the muscles of the body run to, creating a spiderweb-like framework throughout the body. He talked about a mysterious, spooky substance he called “pneumata psychikonm”, carried by the nerves that were behind the activation of the muscles of the body. Equally preposterous, but a plausible theory at that time.

Then somewhere in the middle ages, while the roman empire crashed to the ground and the European thinkers thought endlessly, a Persian polymath Avicenna spent all of his time studying skull fractures, jotting down his findings. He described many neurological phenomena including hallucinations, insomnia, epilepsy, mania, stroke, tremors, vertigo, paralysis, and melancholia. His articles were cumulated into an encyclopaedia that grew popular among universities and surely the students cursed him when they failed their midterms.

Then around the 16th century, a young Flemish anatomist Andreas Vesalius presented a new, highly creative (and ridiculously imaginative) anatomy of the brain that had 7 pairs of “brain nerves” with specialized powers. In the following years, an English doctor Thomas Willis introduced an elaborate anatomy of the brain and nerves that focused on the minute details as opposed to the vaguer ideas of his predecessors. His anatomy of the brain described the structure of the brainstem, the cerebellum, the ventricles, and the cerebral hemispheres. Thomas Willis also discovered a circle of arteries on the base of the brain and called it the circle of Willis.

The modern period saw some huge breakthroughs in the field of neuroscience. In the second half of the eighteenth century, Luigi Galvani, Lucia Galeazzi Galvani, and Giovanni Aldini studied the role of electricity in nerves for the first time. The centre of respiration was found to be in the medulla oblongata when César Julien Jean Legallois observed respiration in animal dissections and lesions. Charles Bell and François Magendie determined the role of ventral roots and posterior roots in the spine to transmit motor impulses and receive sensory signals respectively. They called this the Bell-Magendie Law. 1848 was a terrible year for Phineas Gage who had an iron tamping rod cut through the frontal lobe of his brain in a blasting accident. However, 1848 was a great year for John Martyn Harlow who studied the connection between the prefrontal cortex and executive functions in the tragic case of Phineas Gage. Another patient in 1861 with progressive loss of speech helped a French physician Paul Broca link speech production to a region in the frontal lobe of the dominant hemisphere. This region was then named Broca's area. Carl Wernicke then developed a closely related theory of the specialization of a small area in the temporal lobe in language comprehension. This region was named Wernicke’s Area.

The invention of the microscope brought about a wave of sophisticated research in this field- the intricate details of the electrically active neurons were observed by Golgi. Cajal used Golgi’s ingenious silver chromate staining technique to reveal the neuron as the functional unit of the brain. Gogi and Cajal shared the Nobel prize for their valuable contributions in this field. The term “autonomic” first came into play when a British scientist John Newport Langley classified the connections of nerve fibres to peripheral nerve cells.

Ivan Pavlov was a popular scientist in the 20th century. He dedicated 12 whole years of his life studying temperament, conditioning, and involuntary reflex actions of the brain which earned him the Nobel prize in physiology and medicine in 1904. The study of reflexes began with Vladimir Bekhterev and was carried forward by Charles Scott Sherrington who discovered motor units in the brain in the process. His concepts centred around unitary behaviour of cells activated or inhibited at what he called synapses. Neurotransmitters like dopamine, serotonin, and adrenaline are common terms but the first neurotransmitter was identified in 1915. Henry Hallett Dale identified acetylcholine while observing its actions on heart tissue. Six years later acetylcholine was confirmed to be a neurotransmitter by Otto Loewi. Sensory organs and the function of nerve cells were the main focus of the British physiologist Keith Lucas and his protégé Edgar Adrian. They formulated the all or none principle that stated that muscles contract entirely or not at all.

The action potential is a big deal in neuroscience. It is the basis of the electric activity performed by neurons. Joseph Erlanger and Herbert Gasser were the first to study the mechanism of the action potential that occurred over two phases- a spike followed by an after-spike. They discovered the individual potential for excitability in the different neurons and were awarded the Nobel prize in1944 for their discovery on the directly proportional relationship between that the velocity of action potentials and the diameter of the nerve fibre.

The 20th century saw some major advances in the field of neuroscience and built the foundation for all the unbelievable breakthroughs made in this century. From believing that the brain had nothing to do with intelligence to discovering the molecular makeup of the brain and inventing tools to visualize the brain, the neuroscience society has come a long way, and needless to say, we’re not at the end of the road yet. We have just begun learning the rules of this game, we’re yet to master it.

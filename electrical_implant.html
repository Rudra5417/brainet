Paralysis Reversal: A New Hope

  1. Home
  2. Blog

Electrical implant helps in reversing paralysis.

  • Veena Girijala
  • Jan 1, 2020

Paraplegics who sustained lower spinal cord injuries are now able to walk with the help of crutches due to electrical implants fitted to their spinal cords. Specialists at the Mayo Clinic in Minnesota, and the University of California, Los Angeles, devised this strategy. The teams came up with the idea of implanting an electrode in the epidural space, the area between the dura mater and the vertebral wall, containing fat and small blood vessels. This space is located just outside the dural sac which surrounds the nerve roots and is filled with cerebrospinal fluid.

The electrode was connected to a pulse generator implanted just below the skin of the patientâ€™s abdomen. The team of researchers was able to wirelessly program the pulse generator to provide targeted electrical stimulation to the spinal cord. The electrode did not repair the damage done to the spinal cord but found a way to overcome the obstacle. That was done by stimulating the nerves found in the lower back. The electrode helped in transmitting the signals from the brain to the target muscles. This resulted in the ability to voluntarily control the movements by the patient.But before the electrode was implanted, the patient had to go through 22 sessions of physiotherapy. After the implantation, the patient was placed under rehabilitation. The patient had to go through an intense training regime.

Under observation, the team adjusted and optimized the settings of the electrical implant. By the end of the study, the patient was able to walk about 102 meters which is about the length of a football field. However, all of this was done under electric stimulation. When the electrical implant was switched off, the patient was not able to walk voluntarily. The patient also could not feel anything below the site of their spinal cord injury. Tests on rats suggest that this type of targeted electrical stimulation encourages new nerve pathways to grow to make full use of the remaining spinal nerves.

This case study proved that neurons found below a spinal cord injury were still able to function after paralysis. This gives hope to people faced with paralysis.

It all started with trying to bring inanimate objects “to life”: give them the ability to think and make decisions. Psychology and neuroscience have played fundamental roles in the history of AI. Even though with time, AI has taken a complete turn towards mathematics, the fact that the initial inspiration was taken from neurons still stands. According to AI and Neuroscience: A virtuous circle, throughout the late 20th Century, much of the vital work developing neural networks took place, not in mathematics or physics labs, but psychology and neurophysiology departments. The founding figures of AI: Alan Turing, Walter Pitts, Warren McCulloch, Marvin Minsky, and Geoff Hinton were driven by an eagerness to understand how the brain functions and wanted to mimic the process. Even though AI has come a long way from its prior reliance on neuroscience, it continues to draw inspiration from how the brain works. Machine learning systems are better than humans at finding complex and subtle patterns in vast data sets. (https://theconversation.com/neuroscience-and-artificial-intelligence-can-help-improve-each-other-110869) Artificial neural networks are highly interconnected networks of nodes that accept inputs, recognise underlying relationships in a collection of data through a process that mimics the way the human brain operates and generate outputs. Neuroscience encourages us to design AI algorithms in two ways. One, which tries to emulate human intelligence, and two, to create neural networks that mimic cognitive function. Here we hope to bring together Neuroscience X AI enthusiasts together by giving them a common platform. We will be looking at this intersection from its usual biological aspects and various correlated model architecture. Nuff about this, let’s talk technicality.

Computational Neuroscience is the branch of neuroscience that deals with mathematical models and analysis of data recorded from the brain and the nervous system to understand the principles that govern the development, structure, physiology and cognitive abilities of the branch. An emerging field called "neuromorphic computing" focuses on the design of computational hardware inspired by the human brain.

Cognitive science has developed human cognition computational models which decompose task performance into computational components. These algorithms, however, fall short of human intelligence and are not neurobiologically established. Computational neuroscience has studied how interacting neurons can execute brain computation component functions. However, how individual components work to understand human cognition and action has yet to be clarified. (Kriegeskorte, Nikolaus, and Pamela K. Douglas. "Cognitive computational neuroscience.")

It surveys the historical connexions between the areas of AI and neuroscience. Computer scientists have also developed algorithms for reinforcement learning in artificial systems. These algorithms enable AI systems to learn complex strategies without external instruction, guided instead by reward predictions. This algorithm, a recent breakthrough in computer science, can explain several previously unexplained aspects of reward learning in the brain and opens new research perspectives with regards to the brain's dopamine system, with possible consequences for learning and motivation disorders.
